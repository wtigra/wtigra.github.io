Wafa Tigra - 2018

Multi-contact Electrical Sciatic Nerve Stimulation for Selective Activation of Muscular Functions in rabbits: feasibility study

Experimental Protocol

Acute experimentations have been performed on 5 New Zealand White male adult rabbits (named N1 to N5). The thigh was incised in its upper part and the sciatic nerve separated from its adjacent tissues. A multipolar cuff electrode (4 transversal * 3 longitudinal contacts, 20 mm length) was placed around the sciatic nerve. To record muscular activities elicited by the nerve stimulation, intramuscular electrodes were placed into up to 6 lower limb muscles. Data were acquired via Powerlab (LabChart software, ADinstrument, Austria) and processed off-line (through this algorithm). 7 different profiles of stimulation (cf. Figure 1) were evaluated. Some of these profiles were reproducted on different contacts of the cuff electrode. For each profile of stimulation, the nerve was stimulated at an increasing intensity (up to 1.8 mA). One estimated that a muscle is not recruited when, stimulated at the maximum intensity, its electrical activity recorded is less than 170% of that recorded for the same muscle at rest. Matlab algorithm, allowing to process, from the raw data, recruitment curves can be find here. For each profile of stimulation, 3 indices were calculated: selectivity, efficiency and robustness. These indices correspond respectively to:

  1. the ratio between the recruitment of "m", the muscle of interest and, the sum of recruitment of all muscles whose electrical activity has been recorded,
  2. the amount of charge required for the profil of stimulation studied to achieve a given effect compared to the amount of charge required for the same effect when the nerve was stimulated through the standard profil "Ring Tripolar",
  3. the variation of the selectivity index when the stimulation current increased by 50%.


Results

Intra and inter-fascicular selectivity

On dissection, 3 anatomical fascicles were observed in the 5 animals. Two motors, future tibial and fibular branches of the sciatic nerve, and one sensory (future sural branch).
Although the most effective profile of stimulation was different from one animal to another, we were able to selectively activate each of the two sciatic nerve motor fascicles in all animals involved in the study, without changing the location of the cuff electrode. Indeed, leg flexion and extension were selectively activated. In addition, intra-fascicular selectivity was shown in 3 animals (N1, N2 and N3). This intra-fascicular selectivity could activate alternately 2 agonist muscles and thus, reduce muscle fatigue induced by electrical stimulation.

Selectivity, Efficiency, Robustness

Among the different profiles studied, those named "Transverse Tripolar" were the most selective (high selectivity). Reproduced on the most distal ring of the cuff electrode (nearest nerve bifurcation), they allowed maximal selectivity but at high intensity (=low efficiency). Selectivity window of "1 Cathode, 2 Anodes, Ring", "1 Cathode, Ring Anode", "Longitudinal Tripolar" and "Steering" profiles was generally shorter (=low robustness) and muscle recruitment rate lower than that found for "Transverse Tripolar" profiles. Then, for a target muscle, the selectivity index was reduced. However, energy required to selectively activate the same target muscle (or group of agonist muscles) was generally lower (=high efficiency).

The same pattern of stimulation reproduced on ring A, B or C does not generally induce the same recruitment profile (cf. Figure 2 and Figure 3 for N1).

Fascicular reorganization

An orthodromic "Bipolar Ring" stimulation (with a distance of 10 mm between the anode and the cathode) seemed to be the best way to stimulate a nerve in a non-selective way at a minimum intensity. Finally, the "Tripolar Ring" profile allows to better focus the electric current than the profile "Bipolar Ring" but required more energy without improving the recruitment profile.
