Summary

  • Needs of individuals with tetraplegia and expectations regarding implanted neuroprosthesis
    •

    The purpose of this questionnaire was to highlight the needs of individuals with tetraplegia and their expectations regarding implanted neuroprosthesis. This survey, carried out at Propara clinic (Montpellier, France), also tried to understand reasons that may explain low use of FES neuroprostheses. During interviews, need for FES implanted devices is perceived still relevant and restoring hand grip is considered a priority among individuals with tetraplegia. However, lack of ergonomics, functionnalities offered and/or natural control could explain the low use of these neuroprostheses. Based on these data, clinical trials using multipolar nerve stimulation (via a nerve cuff electrode) and ergonomics control (via sus-lesionals muscular contractions/upper limb movements) have started.


  • Multi-contact Electrical Nerve Stimulation for Selective Activation of Muscular Functions in rabbits: feasibility study
    •

    The entire experimental stimulation setup having never been used, the goal of this part was to validate it on animals. Because of its size and to get closer to per-operative conditions, we chose to stimulate the rabbit's sciatic nerve. Its fascicular organization is different from that of the human upper limb nerves, but its diameter is closer. 5 animals were used during these experiments. The nerve was stimulated at intensities up to 1.8 mA through more than forty different configurations of stimultion. For each configuration of stimulation and for each animal, 3 indices were calculated: selectivity, efficiency and robustness. Although the most effective pattern of stimulation was different from one animal to another, we were able to selectively produce, without moving the cuff electrode, two antagonist mouvements (flexion and extension of the leg) in all animals. Intrafascicular selectivity was found in 3/5 animals.


  • Clinical trial (#2014-A01752-45): Implanted Nerve Electrical Stimulation allows to Selectively Restore Hand and Forearm Movements in Patients with a Complete Tetraplegia
    •

    The purpose of this work was to evaluate whether a multi-contact nerve cuff electrode can selectively activate forearm and hand muscles and restore functional movements in patients with a complete tetraplegia. To evaluate its ability to selectively activate hand functions, we performed acute experiments during a scheduled surgery (muscle-tendon transfer). A multicontact cuff electrode was designed to selectively activate, without damage them, peripheral nerves innervating multiple muscles. Radial or median nerves were stimulated at an intensity up to 2.1 mA (tetanic stimulation used). The multicontact cuff electrode was able to selectively activate muscle/groups of muscles to produce multiple, independent and functional hand and forearm movements. A surface electrical stimulation was performed before the inclusion but did not allow to elicit all those movements.


  • Clinical trial (#2016-A00711-50): Towards an Ergonomic Control of a Hand Neuroprosthesis for Tetraplegic Persons

    • The goal of this study was to evaluate the ability of patients with tetraplegia to control their upper-limb movements/muscular contractions in a directed, fine and graduated way, and know if these signals could be used as an intuitive mode of control of the previous implanted stimulation. The validation was done through a robotic hand and upper limb muscles surface stimulation. All patients were able to control, via their muscular contractions/upper limb movements, the robotic hand or the surface electrical stimulation of their own arm.
