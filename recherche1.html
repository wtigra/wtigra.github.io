Needs of individuals with tetraplegia and expectations regarding implanted neuroprosthesis: survey

The use of Upper Limb (UL) FES devices have undergone significant technological advances until the abrupt stop of the Freehand® system’s [1] in 2001; since then, UL FES devices have been sidelined. In this survey, I aimed to determine whether this technique should be updated. To identify the real needs and expectations of patients with a tetraplegia, I developed an experience-based questionnaire with the help of both a clinical rehabilitation team and one patient who has had C6 level SCI since 1999 and had used the Freehand® UL neuroprosthesis. This questionnaire was administered to individuals with tetraplegia and the following inclusion criteria: adult subject, traumatic SCI, complete motor (AIS A or B) tetraplegia, and hospitalized or undergoing care.

Demographic data

Forty tetraplegic patients were identified during the period from February 2013 to June 2013. Thirty-one of them met the inclusion criteria and were selected. The distribution of neurological levels is consistent with spinal cord lesion description standards [2].

Functional data

All subjects questioned used human help to assist them in tasks of daily life, especially in areas requiring reliable gestures. Nearly 80% of them solicited it for control of vesico-sphincterian and intestinal functions, 84% for food intake, 80% for transfers, and 80% for personal grooming. Only one patient used an animal aid, and only one patient used the Freehand® neuroprosthesis to help with daily living tasks. All patients needed technical aids for the control of vesico-sphincterian and intestinal functions and displacement (electric/manual wheelchairs). The person using the Freehand® neuroprosthesis to regain hand mobility stated that he limited his use of human aids for food intake, writing, and taking care of his appearance.

Expectations and needs of tetraplegic persons

Among the tasks proposed, the patients chose three tasks that they placed among their recovery priorities. Being able to perform personal grooming alone and eat without the help of a third party emerged as the main priorities. The use of an FES device or a functional surgery is particularly motivated by a gain in autonomy (74% all levels of injury) and the respect of intimacy (e.g. to be able to wash and catheterize alone, for 45%).

Acceptability of implanted FES device to allow hand movements

To recover hand movements, more than three-quarters of tetraplegic patients are willing to accept functional surgery, but if there are not enough muscles under voluntary control to perform neuro-tendinous surgeries, 61% of them are willing to benefit from a neuroprosthesis implantation. Some patients (10%) did not give their opinion and wished to have more time to decide, while 29% of them give a categorical refusal. Seniority of accident also emerged as an input.
