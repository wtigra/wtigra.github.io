Wafa Tigra

PhD: clinical research, medical devices

Implanted Functional Electrical Stimulation (FES) is used in a growing number of applications, including cardiac stimulation (pacemaker), deep brain stimulation or acoustic stimulation (cochlear implants). Attempts to use this technology have been made for functional rehabilitation in patients with spinal cord injury. However, for complex motor functions, this technology has rarely reached the stage of industrial production, with the exception of the Freehand® system (NeuroControl Corporation, USA) for a short period (1997-2001). This system used 8 epimysial electrodes for muscle stimulation and an external shoulder controller to trig two hand movements (palmar and lateral grasp). Although functional benefits of this system were important, this device had deficiencies in command orders and functionalities offered. Some limitations may be corrected in the light of recent technological advances.


Summary of my PhD research work

My PhD thesis work, based on the success of the Freehand® system, proposed an original approach built on (i) implanted selective nerve electrical stimulation (via a multicontact cuff electrode) to restore functional hand/forearm movements in persons with a tetraplegia and (ii) supra-lesional muscle signals as an ergonomic control of this stimulation. Indeed, compared to neuromuscular stimulation, nerve stimulation requires ten times less energy than muscular stimulation and reduces the number of implanted electrodes. An ergonomic interface, closely related to the remaining abilities of individuals with tetraplegia would allow a wider use of these FES devices.
Clinical trials realized in per-operatory and ambulatory conditions have been set up to validate key points of this approach (neural stimulation, EMG control). Animal experiments preceded these trials.
Thus, based on functional specifications revealed at the end of my PhD thesis work, it has been demonstrated that a fully implantable system using nerve multipolar electrical stimulation and EMG control, for restoring hand/forearm movements, could be developed. A long term clinical trial should be conducted in the future. Part of contributions resulting from my PhD thesis work is presented in section Publications.

This work was funded by MXM company, Sophia Antipolis and carried out at INRIA, Propara private hospital and Beausoleil private hospital, Montpellier.
