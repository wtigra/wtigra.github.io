Clinical Trial #II: Towards an Ergonomic Control of a Hand Neuroprosthesis for Tetraplegic Persons (#2016-A00711-50)

The goal of this study was to evaluate the ability of tetraplegic patients to control their upper-limb movements and muscular contractions in a directed, fine and graduated way, and know if these signals could be used as an intuitive mode of control of the previous implanted stimulation.
The validation was done through a robotic hand (Shadow Robot Company, London, UK), which reproduce closely the kinematics and dexterity of the human hand, with which the patient interacted via his movements and residual muscle contractions. An experimental validation based on surface electrical stimulation of forearm muscles concluded this step. A finite state machine was used, to move from one (open) state to another (closed) state.
We demonstrated in the 13 tetraplegic subjects who participated in the experiments, a combination of muscles that can be used to easily control a robotic hand/his own hand (via surface electrical stimulation). Continuous or gradual contractions of these residual muscles can be maintained without any prior learning or training. In addition, an interesting property of the proposed interface is that non-functional muscles, in the context of movements produced, can be transformed into functional muscles in the context of assistive technology. However, control modalities and preferential muscles were "subject-dependent".



For more details about the EMG/upper limb movements protocol and results cf. Chapter 6 of my PhD thesis (in French) and A novel EMG interface for individuals with tetraplegia to pilot robot hand grasping.
